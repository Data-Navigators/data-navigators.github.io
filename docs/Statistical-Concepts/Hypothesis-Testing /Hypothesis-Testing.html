Hypothesis-Testing

Introduction​

Hypothesis testing is a statistical method that helps you decide if there's enough evidence in a sample of data to infer that a certain condition is true for the entire population. It’s widely used in research to validate or invalidate assumptions.

Key Concepts​

1. Null and Alternative Hypotheses​

  • Null Hypothesis (H₀): The default assumption that there is no relationship or effect.
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H₁): The assumption that contradicts the null hypothesis, proposing there is a relationship or effect.

For example, in a retail setting, you may hypothesize:

  • H₀: There is no significant difference in sales between male and female customers.
  • H₁: There is a significant difference in sales between male and female customers.

2. p-value​

The p-value tells you the probability of obtaining results at least as extreme as the observed results, under the assumption that the null hypothesis is correct. It helps to determine whether to reject the null hypothesis.

  • Low p-value (< 0.05): Strong evidence against the null hypothesis, so you reject it.
  • High p-value (> 0.05): Weak evidence against the null hypothesis, so you fail to reject it.

3. t-test​

The t-test is used to determine if there is a significant difference between the means of two groups.

  • Independent t-test: Used when comparing means from two different groups (e.g., comparing average sales between male and female customers).
  • Paired t-test: Used when comparing means from the same group at different times (e.g., before and after a marketing campaign).

4. Chi-Square Test​

The Chi-Square test is used to determine if there is a significant association between two categorical variables (e.g., product category and customer gender).

  • Chi-Square Goodness-of-Fit Test: Used to determine if sample data matches a population.
  • Chi-Square Test of Independence: Used to determine if there is an association between two categorical variables.

5. Z-test​

The Z-test is used when the sample size is large (> 30) and you want to compare the sample mean to the population mean or compare two sample means.

6. ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)​

ANOVA is used when comparing the means of three or more groups. It tests the null hypothesis that all group means are equal.

Step-by-Step Guide for Hypothesis Testing​

  1. State the Hypotheses: Define your null and alternative hypotheses.
  2. Set the Significance Level (α): Commonly, α = 0.05.
  3. Collect Data: Gather your sample data.
  4. Perform the Test:
    • For numerical data, use t-test or ANOVA.
    • For categorical data, use Chi-Square test.
  5. Calculate the p-value: Using statistical software or a formula.
  6. Make a Decision:
    • Reject H₀ if p-value < α.
    • Fail to reject H₀ if p-value ≥ α.
  7. Draw Conclusions: Interpret the results in the context of your hypothesis.
